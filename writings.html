Writings on Justice, Race, and Non-Violence

Reflections on affirmative action, voting rights, and civil disobedience.

On Affirmative Action: The Reality Behind Initiative 200

I was hoping I would not have to write this letter. For three months, I campaigned against Washington State's Initiative 200. We lost by a wide margin. I am surprised by the number of voters who rejected affirmative action for women and minorities.

We must continue the struggle. Dr. Martin Luther King's dream remains unfulfilled. We hope for a color-blind society, but reality proves we still judge by skin color. As citizens in a democracy, we have a duty to demand justice. If we do not stand against injustice, we are accomplices.

"I wish more people had a chip on their shoulder and protested when Nazi Germany exterminated six million Jews."

Tuan Tran

Proponents claim the U.S. is now color-blind. Yet, Amnesty International condemns the U.S. for human rights violations against people of color:

  • Selective Policing & Brutality: In April 1998, New Jersey troopers fired on Black and Latino drivers after a minor accident, wounding three.
  • Death Penalty Bias: In McCleskey v. Kemp (1987), the Supreme Court conceded race influences death penalty sentencing. Blacks are four times more likely to receive the death penalty than whites.
  • Incarceration: Blacks are 12% of the population but 42% of death row inmates.

By accepting institutionalized racism, the Court repeats the error of Dred Scott v. Sandford (1856), which deemed Black people property. We must coordinate efforts to combat this systemic inequality.

On Voting: The Fundamental Right for Equality

"We hold these truths to be self-evident, that all men are created equal..." — Thomas Jefferson.

Vietnamese refugees have endured immense suffering since the fall of Saigon (April 30, 1975). As we approach anniversaries of our arrival, we must reflect on our place in the Civil Rights struggle.

The Declaration of Independence (1776) guaranteed equality, but:

  • Amendment XV (1870) granted voting rights to people of color.
  • Amendment XIX (1920) granted women the right to vote.
  • Full exercise of these rights only came around 1960 through the Civil Rights Movement.

For Vietnamese-Americans, the vote is our tool for full equality. Rather than living in isolation, we must vote to achieve integration and help America move past the Vietnam War toward peace.

On Civil Disobedience: The Power of Ahimsa (Non-Violence)

The 20th century was defined by colonialism and war, yet also by the transformative power of love in protest. To understand civil disobedience, we must look to Gandhi and Martin Luther King Jr.

Both were men of religious conviction—not necessarily tied to a specific dogma, but to the truth that love is the absence of violence.

A non-violent act aims not just to gain material benefits, but to transform the heart of society. Without love, protest becomes a vicious cycle where the victim becomes the perpetrator.

"Go find an orphan Muslim boy... whose parents were killed by Hindus, and raise that boy as though he is your own son."

Gandhi's reply to a Hindu man seeking revenge

True protest is not shouting or vindictiveness. It is the willingness to sacrifice oneself to break the cycle of hatred.
